# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

Indie writers who already post on X, Threads, and Substack — Substackers and build-in-public solo creators are the core. They write well but aren't designers, and they want every post they publish to look branded and native to the platform it came from without opening Figma or fighting fonts. Their context on the landing page is a quick, skeptical visit ("is this actually faster than what I do now?"), usually from a link in a post or bio. The job to be done: go from "I wrote a good post" to "I have an Instagram-ready image of it" in seconds.

## Product Purpose

Notes2Pic turns a written post into a clean, on-brand, Instagram-ready image — short-post cards, medium-form quotes, and multi-slide carousels — with no blank canvas and no design skill required. It exists because writers lose the momentum of a good post when turning it into a visual is slow and ugly. Success is a visitor watching the transformation, believing "that's my post, and it looks great," and starting a free export within the first session.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary and secondary CTA: Primary is **Get lifetime ($10, founding-member price)**. Secondary is **Start free** for visitors not ready to pay — free editing/preview, sign-in only to export.
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: "It turns my posts into branded images in seconds."
- Belief ladder: (1) I understand what this does at a glance — it's my post, as an image. (2) The output actually looks good, not template-y. (3) It's genuinely faster than what I do now. (4) The lifetime deal is a real, limited founding-member offer worth locking in over waiting.
- Proof on hand: Pre-launch — no testimonials or user counts yet. Lean on the live demo of the transformation and the real example images the tool produces as the proof, plus the honest founding-member framing (first 20 buyers at $10, then $17). Do not fabricate testimonials, logos, or user numbers.

## Brand Personality

Warm, indie, honest. A human, build-in-public voice — plain-spoken, a little scrappy, never corporate or hypey. Confident about the one thing it does well, upfront about what it deliberately doesn't do (no scraping, manual by design). The interface should feel like a tool made by a writer for writers: friendly and text-forward, with the craft of a premium product but none of the enterprise polish that reads as impersonal.

## Anti-references

Explicitly NOT generic blue-SaaS: no blue-accent-on-white template look, no small uppercase tracked eyebrow above every section, no 4-up grid of identical icon-heading-body feature cards, no gradient CTA band. This is the current design and the thing we are moving away from. Reference feel to borrow from: buttondown.com (warm, minimal, writer-first restraint) and Raycast (playful-premium, demo-forward, strong personality) — warmth and demo-led clarity, not their exact palettes.

## Design Principles

- **Show the transformation, don't describe it.** The hero proves the value by demonstrating post → image live; copy supports the demo, never replaces it.
- **The output is the argument.** Real example images the tool makes are the strongest proof we have pre-launch; feature them, don't hide them behind icons.
- **Honest over hyped.** Founding-member urgency is real and framed plainly; no fake social proof, no invented metrics.
- **Writer-first warmth.** Text-forward, human voice, and typography that respects reading — this is a tool for people who care about words.
- **Earn every section.** No scaffolding-by-reflex (eyebrows, numbered markers, identical card grids); each section exists because it moves a specific belief.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Standard web accessibility: body text ≥4.5:1 contrast (the current muted-gray-on-tint is a known risk to fix), visible focus states, keyboard-navigable CTAs, and a `prefers-reduced-motion` alternative for the demo animation and any reveal motion (crossfade or static final state — the transformation must be understandable without motion).
